# FMGE Strategy Guide

FMGE is a screening exam, not a ranking exam — you're not competing against other candidates, you're clearing a fixed bar of 150/300 (50%), with no sectional cutoffs and no negative marking. That single fact should shape almost every prep decision: breadth beats perfection, guessing is always better than leaving a blank, and time is best spent where marks-per-hour is highest rather than chasing exhaustive mastery of any one subject. The exam draws heavily from repeated, predictable high-yield facts across previous papers rather than obscure edge cases, so recognizing patterns matters more than encyclopedic depth.

- **Attack by marks-per-hour, not alphabetically.** Medicine (33), Surgery (32), PSM (30), and OBG (30) are 125 of 300 marks — 42% of the exam in four subjects. Never let a low-weight subject (Radiotherapy, Anaesthesiology, Orthopaedics — 5 marks each) eat time that a high-weight subject needs, even if the low-weight one feels more "fun" to study.

- **Never leave a question blank.** With zero negative marking, an educated guess has pure upside. In the final taper phase, drill yourself on elimination strategy (rule out 2 wrong options, guess between the remaining 2) so this becomes reflexive under time pressure, not something you have to think about on exam day.

- **Previous Years' Questions (PYQs) are the syllabus, in practice.** FMGE recycles high-yield concepts across cycles far more than most exams. Solve the last 8-10 years of PYQs subject-wise as you finish each subject in Phases 1-4, not as an afterthought in Phase 5 — this tells you which topics within a subject are actually tested versus theoretically in-syllabus.

- **Treat your "easy" tier as a revision problem, not a learning problem.** Surgery, OBG, PSM, ENT, Ophthalmology, and Forensic Medicine are worth 132 marks combined and already comfortable for you. The failure mode here isn't ignorance, it's carelessness from over-familiarity — timed practice sets with review of every wrong answer (even "silly" ones) matter more than re-reading textbooks.

- **Your hard tier needs first-principles time, not just more hours.** Medicine, Pathology, Physiology, Anatomy, Pharmacology, and Pediatrics total 118 marks and are where knowledge gaps will actually cost you the pass. Budget deep-focus study blocks (not fragmented sessions) for these, especially Medicine and Pathology, which integrate with almost every clinical subject.

- **Use full-length timed mocks as a diagnostic tool, not just a scoring exercise.** After every mock, log every wrong answer into three buckets: didn't know it, knew it but misread the question, and guessed wrong. "Didn't know it" drives what to restudy; "misread" drives exam-technique fixes; "guessed wrong" is often fine to ignore. This is the fastest way to convert mock scores into real point gains.

- **Protect the final 10 days.** The taper phase (late Dec – Jan 8) should have zero new content — only revision of your own notes, one final mock, and deliberate sleep-schedule alignment to your exam slot time. Cramming new subjects in the last week reliably lowers scores, not raises them, because it displaces retention of what you already know.

- **150/300 is the finish line, not a target to exceed by a huge margin.** If mock scores show you comfortably clearing 165-170+ with weeks still on the calendar, don't keep grinding the same subjects for marginal gains — redistribute that time to your weakest-performing high-weight subject instead.
